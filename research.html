Understanding Digital and Non-Digital Addictions: Determinants and Interrelations

Selin Yardimci ‚Äî latest draft: Nov 2025

We examine the structural determinants and interdependencies of digital and non-digital addictions using nationally representative French survey data. Exploiting a maximum simulated likelihood estimation of a multivariate probit model with multiple sample selections, we correct for selection into addiction by conditioning on initial engagement. To assess behavioral spillovers across domains, we estimate a recursive multivariate probit model over five addictive behaviors: smartphone use, social media use, video gaming, smoking, and alcohol consumption. Results reveal significant selection bias in digital and substance-based addictions, with risk tolerance and fear of missing out predicting digital overuse, and household size and age reducing vulnerability. Recursive estimates suggest that smoking increases the probability of both social media and video gaming addictions, while video gaming substitutes for alcohol addiction. Smartphone and social media addictions exhibit strong mutual reinforcement. These patterns indicate that interventions targeting one addiction may affect the incidence of others, depending on the direction of their structural linkage.

Keywords: Multivariate Probit Models; Normal Distribution; Econometric Simulation; Microeconometrics; Addiction

Recommendation System, Pricing Algorithm and Price Discrimination

with A. Brandenburg ‚Äî latest draft: Dec 2025

Price algorithms and recommendation systems play an important role in online marketplaces. We model two firms, each relying on algorithmic pricing, competing on a platform endowed with a recommendation system. Consumers are of two types, Loyals and Switchers, with recommendations only affecting the latter type's shopping behavior. We show that there is a minimum quality threshold for the recommendation system at which it is optimal to discriminate on price in the duopolistic cases. As recommendation quality decreases, consumers become more captive to their initially favored firm, making them less sensitive to price changes. Price discrimination becomes less effective in driving competitive advantages and lower profits compared to uniform pricing. In line with the literature, price discrimination is always profitable in the monopolistic case. We run simulations using reinforcement learning algorithms (Q-learning and Sarsa) to study the collusion ability of these pricing algorithms. We show that the degree of collusion depends negatively on the quality of the recommendation system because of the increase in competitive pressure resulting from a proficient recommendation system.

Keywords: Algorithms; Recommendation systems; Platforms; Collusion

Intrinsic Motivation and Altruism under Rational Inattention: Do We Perform Better When We Care?

with Z. Wang, A. Brandenburg ‚Äî latest draft: Dec 2025

Under the rational inattention framework, we explore the relationship between intrinsic motivation, altruism, information (attention) cost, and decision performance using perceptual tasks. We find counterintuitive results: higher intrinsic motivation leads subjects to perform worse. Furthermore, a high level of altruism is associated with lower decision performance when information acquisition benefits others. These observations are attributed to a positive relationship between altruism, intrinsic motivation, and information cost. Our findings highlight the complex trade-offs between intrinsic motivation, altruism, and cognitive limitations. They challenge the common assumption that increased motivation may lead to better outcomes. Meanwhile, we find that the net expected utility and the cost of information acquisition do not vary across different decision contexts. These results suggest that cognitive constraints may depend less on how subjects are paid and more on how the experiment is designed.

Keywords: Altruism; Rational inattention; Intrinsic motivations; Perceptual tasks
